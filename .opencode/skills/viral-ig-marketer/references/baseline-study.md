# Baseline Study — How to Learn From What Works

Before generating content, study what's already working in the niche. This isn't a one-time task — revisit every few cycles as trends shift.

---

## Finding Content to Study

**Start with the niche, not specific accounts.** Search for top-performing content in sobriety/alcohol-free living. Look at what's getting views *right now*, not what was popular 6 months ago.

**Where to look:**
- Web search for "top [niche] creators on [platform]" or "viral [niche] content 2026"
- Instagram explore/hashtag pages (via Graph API search when available)
- Cross-platform: TikTok creators often cross-post to Reels

**What makes a good reference:**
- High views relative to follower count (a 10K-follower account with 500K views found something that works)
- Content you could structurally replicate (face-to-camera personal stories are useful for hook research but not format research)
- Recent (last 3 months) — platform algorithms change fast

---

## What to Extract From Each Piece of Content

Don't just watch — decompose. For every video worth studying, pull out:

1. **The hook (first 1-3 seconds):** What made you stop? Was it a bold claim? A question? A visual pattern interrupt? The hook is the most important element — everything else is irrelevant if nobody stops scrolling.

2. **Emotional register:** Is it vulnerable? Aspirational? Informational? Provocative? The register determines who engages and how. Vulnerable content gets shares. Informational gets saves. Provocative gets comments.

3. **Structure and pacing:** How many scenes? How long per scene? Where does the energy peak? Good reels have rhythm — they don't just present information linearly.

4. **The CTA:** What's the ask? Follow? Comment? Share? Save? The best CTAs feel natural, not bolted on. "Tag someone who needs to hear this" works because it's emotional, not transactional.

5. **Replicability:** Can we produce something structurally similar without a face, without a film crew, without a huge budget? Score 1-5. Focus energy on formats scoring 3+.

---

## Patterns to Look For

**The 10x question:** Same creator, same production quality, but one video gets 50K views and another gets 5M. Why? The answer is almost always the hook or the topic, not the production.

**Format vs. substance:** A beautiful video with a weak hook dies. An ugly video with a magnetic hook goes viral. Prioritize substance research (hooks, topics, emotional triggers) over format research (fonts, transitions, color grading).

**Face vs. faceless:** Most viral content in personal transformation niches uses a face. As a brand account without one, we need to understand which *topics and hooks* translate to faceless formats and which don't. Not everything transfers.

**Seasonal and cultural hooks:** Dry January, Sober October, New Year's resolutions — these create massive temporary interest spikes. Plan content around them when relevant.

---

## Turning Research Into Action

After studying 5-10 pieces of content, you should be able to answer:

1. **Which 3 topics consistently get the most engagement in this niche?**
2. **What hook structures work best?** (question? bold claim? before/after? curiosity gap?)
3. **What emotional register resonates most?** (and does it match our brand?)
4. **What's the minimum production quality threshold?** (below which content looks amateur, above which you're over-investing)

Write these findings into `virality-model.md` — that's where the agent's content intuition lives. Don't just list what you found; distill the *principles* so they apply to future content, not just the next post.

---

## When to Re-Study

- After 5 cycles with declining performance
- When entering a new content format (carousels → reels, reels → stories)
- When a competitor suddenly breaks out (understand what changed)
- Monthly at minimum — the algorithm and trends don't stand still
